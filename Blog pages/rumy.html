Visit The Rumi Darwaza and Explore Its Nearby Attractions

12 Sep 2020

09mins

BY MANIRATNA ROY

The Rumi Darwaza, located in Lucknow, India, is an iconic gateway that stands as a symbol of the city's rich cultural and architectural heritage. Built in 1784 under the patronage of Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula, this majestic structure is a prime example of Awadhi architecture, reflecting the grandeur and elegance of the Nawabi era. Inspired by the Sublime Porte in Istanbul, the Rumi Darwaza rises to a height of 60 feet, adorned with intricate floral patterns and ornamental designs that showcase the artistry of the time. Serving as an entrance to the old city of Lucknow, it once marked the divide between the city’s bustling streets and the royal areas. Constructed as part of a famine relief program, it not only provided employment to thousands of people but also became a lasting symbol of generosity and resilience. Today, the Rumi Darwaza stands proudly as a beloved landmark, inviting visitors to marvel at its beauty and relive the splendor of Lucknow’s glorious past.

Rumi Darwaza stands as a symbol of Lucknow’s rich cultural heritage, representing the grandeur of the Nawabi era known for its art, poetry, and hospitality. The area around the monument reflects the city’s vibrant traditions, from classical music and dance to Urdu poetry and storytelling. The architectural beauty of Rumi Darwaza, with its Mughal and Awadhi influences, is a testament to Lucknow’s artistic legacy. Visitors can experience the warmth and tehzeeb (etiquette) of the city, which is deeply rooted in its Nawabi past.

1. Awadhi Cuisine: A Taste of Lucknow’s Rich Heritage

The culinary experience near Rumi Darwaza is a delight for food lovers, as Lucknow is renowned for its mouth-watering Awadhi cuisine. The streets around the monument are filled with eateries serving iconic dishes such as Tunday Kababi’s famous galouti kebabs, Lucknawi biryani, sheermal, and nihari-kulcha. Don’t miss trying kebabs from Rahim’s or indulging in sweet treats like makhan malai and kulfi from local vendors. The rich flavors and slow-cooked delicacies are a must-try for anyone visiting the area.

Awadhi Cuisine

Awadhi cuisine is a rich and royal culinary tradition from the city of Lucknow, known for its aromatic spices, slow-cooked dishes, and Mughal-inspired flavors. It evolved during the Nawabi era, blending Persian, Central Asian, and North Indian cooking techniques. The cuisine is known for using techniques like Dum Pukht (slow cooking) and Mughlai tandoor preparations, resulting in dishes with deep, layered flavors

2. Nawabi Culture : A Blend of Art, Music, and Poetry

The Nawabi culture of Lucknow is a rich blend of art, music, and poetry, reflecting the grandeur and refinement of the Nawabs of Awadh during the 18th and 19th centuries. This era saw the flourishing of artistic expression through iconic architectural marvels like Bara Imambara, Rumi Darwaza, and Chota Imambara, along with the rise of the delicate craft of chikankari embroidery, now renowned worldwide. The Nawabi courts became a hub for Hindustani classical music, nurturing styles like Thumri, Ghazal, and Qawali, while also fostering the growth of the Kathak dance form. Poetry, especially Urdu Shayari, thrived during this period, with legendary poets like ,Mir Taqi Mir and Mirza Dabeer captivating audiences through their verses at grand Mushiaras (poetry gatherings). Together, these artistic and cultural expressions shaped the identity of Lucknow, making the Nawabi Culture a timeless symbol of elegance, creativity, and hospitality.

Kathak: A Dance Form of Lucknow

Lucknow is renowned for Kathak, a classical dance form celebrated for its graceful expressions, rhythmic footwork, and storytelling through gestures, deeply rooted in the city's Nawabi culture. The city also boasts Chikankari embroidery, a traditional hand-embroidery style known for its intricate patterns, reflecting the artistic legacy of Lucknow’s craftsmen.

3. Chota Imambara: A Masterpiece of Architecture

Chota Imambara, also known as Husainabad Imambara, was built in 1838 by Nawab Muhammad Ali Shah of Awadh as a congregation hall for Shia Muslims and his family’s mausoleum. The monument showcases stunning Persian and Mughal architectural styles, with grand domes, minarets, and exquisite calligraphy. Inside, the halls dazzle with Belgian chandeliers, mirrors, and tazias (replicas of Imam Hussain’s shrine). The Imambara, nicknamed the ‘Palace of Lights,’ glows magnificently when illuminated during Muharram.

Chota Imambara

The Chota Imambara complex offers visitors serene surroundings with well-manicured gardens and reflective ponds. Nearby stands the iconic Satkhanda, an unfinished four-story watchtower, initially intended as a seven-story observatory. The tranquil Husainabad Pond enhances the monument’s charm, providing picturesque views and opportunities for photography. The area becomes particularly vibrant during festivals, with beautiful illuminations. Together, these attractions make Chota Imambara a centerpiece of Lucknow’s rich Nawabi heritage and cultural grandeur.

4.Bada Imambada : Symbol of Lucknow's Architectural Grandeur

Bara Imambara, built in 1784 by Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula, is a symbol of Lucknow’s Nawabi heritage and architectural brilliance. The grand structure, constructed without beams, features the world’s largest arched hall. The complex includes the famous Bhool Bhulaiya, a mysterious labyrinth with 1,000 interconnected passages. Built during a famine, it provided employment to thousands, blending charity with legacy, making it a monument of both history and purpose.

Bada Imambara

The Bara Imambara complex offers more than just its central hall. Visitors can explore the Shahi Baoli, a stepwell with an intriguing optical illusion, and the Asafi Mosque, an architectural gem within the complex. The thrilling experience of navigating the Bhool Bhulaiya (maze) is a highlight for tourists. Surrounded by lush gardens, the site is perfect for exploration, blending mystery, history, and Nawabi charm in one remarkable location.

5. Husainabad Clock Tower: Symbol of Colonial Grandeur

The Husainabad Clock Tower, built in 1881 by Nawab Nasir-ud-din Haider, stands as a magnificent symbol of Victorian and Gothic architecture in Lucknow. Constructed to commemorate the arrival of Sir George Couper, the first Lieutenant Governor of the United Provinces of Awadh, it is the tallest clock tower in India, reaching 221 feet. Its design, inspired by London’s Big Ben, features a 14-foot-long pendulum and intricately designed clock hands shaped like flowers.

Husainabad Clock Tower

The Clock Tower is set amidst the beautiful Husainabad Complex, surrounded by lush gardens and the serene Husainabad Pond, where visitors can enjoy boat rides. Nearby attractions include the Chota Imambara, Satkhanda, and the Husainabad Picture Gallery, making it a central spot in Lucknow’s heritage trail. The tower is especially captivating in the evening when illuminated, creating a stunning reflection in the pond and offering a perfect blend of history and scenic charm.
